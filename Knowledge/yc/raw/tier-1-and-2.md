# DALTON CALDWELL — YC APPLICATION & INTERVIEW

\#\# SOURCE 1: DALTON CALDWELL — YC APPLICATION & INTERVIEW SYSTEM

\#\#\# SOURCE INFO  
\- \*\*Author:\*\* Dalton Caldwell (YC Managing Director, Head of Admissions, 21 batches, 6,500+ office hours, reviewed tens of thousands of applications)  
\- \*\*Authority Level:\*\* PRIMARY — HIGHEST for application evaluation  
\- \*\*Sources:\*\* TechCrunch interview, YC Interview Guide, Lenny's Podcast, Startup School talk

\#\#\# HOW YC ACTUALLY READS APPLICATIONS

\*\*First thing Dalton looks at: THE TEAM\*\*  
\- "The first thing I look at when I read an application is the team. What I'm looking for is technical excellence on the team"  
\- Dalton's \#1 tip: make it clear at least one person can build the product  
\- He reads dozens, sometimes hundreds of applications with the same idea — what differentiates is the TEAM, not the idea

\*\*What makes YC stop reading:\*\*  
\- Lying or misrepresenting (instant kill — "You wouldn't believe the stuff people put in applications")  
\- No one technical on the team  
\- Can't explain what the company does simply  
\- Overly long, novel-length applications (conciseness wins)

\*\*What makes YC keep reading:\*\*  
\- Clear evidence someone can build the product  
\- Progress since applying (launched, got users, grew revenue)  
\- Thoughtful answer to "Why did you pick this idea?"  
\- Hacked a non-computer system (shows resourcefulness — "a strong answer can rescue a weak application")

\*\*Application truths most founders don't know:\*\*  
\- More than half of each batch applied before and got rejected at least once  
\- You don't need warm intros — the system is designed for complete strangers  
\- Don't obsess over the application — "I haven't seen a great correlation between folks that spend tons of time on their application and actually getting in"  
\- Cold emails and decks sent outside the application are archived unread  
\- \~1/3 of each batch applied in a previous batch — showing progress between applications is one of the strongest positive signals

\#\#\# YC INTERVIEW — THE ACTUAL FORMAT

\*\*Structure:\*\*  
\- 10 minutes over Zoom  
\- All founders must be present  
\- 2-3 YC Partners on the call  
\- Partners have already read the application

\*\*The first question (always):\*\* "What is your company working on?"  
\- This is the most basic investor question — and most founders can't answer it clearly  
\- Explain in a few simple, jargon-free sentences  
\- Don't prepare speeches or screencasts — they want a conversation

\*\*What interviewers actually assess:\*\*  
\- Can you explain what you do simply?  
\- Do you know your users and metrics cold?  
\- Are you honest about challenges? (Candid \> glib dismissal of problems)  
\- Can you demo what you've built?  
\- Do all founders participate, or does one person dominate?  
\- Are you earnest and natural, or rehearsed and awkward?

\*\*Questions YC asks if you've launched:\*\*  
\- Where do new users come from?  
\- What is your growth like?  
\- How much are your users using the product, and do they stick around?  
\- What are your unit economics?  
\- What makes new users try you? Why do the reluctant ones hold back?  
\- What are the top things users want?  
\- What has surprised you about user behavior?

\*\*Counter-intuitive interview advice:\*\*  
\- Don't do mock interview prep — it often makes founders worse (over-rehearsed, awkward)  
\- Don't prepare presentations — they want spontaneous conversation  
\- Instead of rehearsing, MAKE PROGRESS — launch, get users, grow between application and interview  
\- "Demonstrating you can move fast and make quick progress is the most surefire way to impress"  
\- It's OK to not know an answer — "A smart person trying sincerely to answer an unexpected question can lead to a great discussion"

\#\#\# DALTON ON FOUNDER TRAITS

\*\*The defining trait of successful founders:\*\*  
\- Not a specific personality type  
\- Profound self-belief — unwavering conviction in their startup's potential  
\- Refuse to accept failure — they persuade others of their inevitable success  
\- "Just don't die" mindset

\*\*On tar pit ideas (Dalton's key concept):\*\*  
\- Ideas that receive initial positive feedback but have hidden structural problems  
\- They SOUND good, people nod along, but there's a reason nobody has succeeded  
\- Examples: social apps for niche demographics, "Uber for X" without network effects  
\- First defense: Google the idea and study why others failed

# PAUL GRAHAM — "HOW TO GET STARTUP IDEAS

\#\# SOURCE 2: PAUL GRAHAM — "HOW TO GET STARTUP IDEAS"

\#\#\# SOURCE INFO  
\- \*\*Author:\*\* Paul Graham (YC co-founder)  
\- \*\*Authority Level:\*\* PRIMARY — HIGHEST  
\- \*\*Date:\*\* November 2012  
\- \*\*URL:\*\* paulgraham.com/startupideas.html

\#\#\# CORE THESIS

\*\*"The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas. It's to look for problems, preferably problems you have yourself."\*\*

\#\#\# THE THREE QUALITIES OF THE BEST STARTUP IDEAS

The very best startup ideas have three things in common:  
1\. \*\*Something the founders themselves want\*\* — you ARE the target user  
2\. \*\*Something they themselves can build\*\* — technical capability to execute  
3\. \*\*Something that few others realize are worth doing\*\* — contrarian insight

\#\#\# THE "LIVE IN THE FUTURE" FRAMEWORK

\- "Live in the future, then build what's missing"  
\- Be at the leading edge of a rapidly changing field  
\- Ideas appear when you're working at the frontier of something — they're noticed, not brainstormed  
\- Microsoft, Apple, Yahoo, Google, Facebook — none were supposed to be companies. They grew out of things founders built because there seemed a gap in the world

\#\#\# ORGANIC vs MADE-UP IDEAS

\*\*Organic ideas (GOOD):\*\*  
\- Grow out of your own life and work experience  
\- You notice a problem because you live with it daily  
\- Feel obvious in retrospect — "of course someone should build this"  
\- Usually start narrow and expand

\*\*Made-up ideas (DANGEROUS):\*\*  
\- Decided from afar — "this market needs X"  
\- Sound plausible at a distance but don't solve a real problem up close  
\- Often dressed up as "Uber for X" or "social network for Y"  
\- Can fool the founder because they sound smart

\#\#\# KEY FILTERS TO TURN OFF

1\. \*\*Schlep filter:\*\* Unconsciously avoiding ideas that involve hard, messy work (see Source 8\)  
2\. \*\*Unsexy filter:\*\* Avoiding boring-sounding ideas in unsexy industries — but these often have less competition and real demand  
3\. \*\*Competition filter:\*\* Assuming that existing competitors means the market is taken — "it's exceptionally rare for startups to be killed by competitors"

\#\#\# WELL OF IDEAS (From the essay)

\- \*\*A crowded market is a GOOD sign\*\* — it indicates demand, and it means no existing solution is good enough  
\- \*\*"If you have something that no competitor does and that some subset of users urgently need, you have a beachhead"\*\*  
\- \*\*"The initial group of users is usually small"\*\* — if something large numbers urgently needed could be built with v1 effort, it would already exist  
\- \*\*Startups serving a small initial market look unpromising\*\* — but this is actually ideal (PayPal → eBay sellers, Facebook → Harvard)

\#\#\# EVALUATION CRITERIA (Direct from PG)

| Signal | Good | Bad |  
|--------|------|-----|  
| Origin | Noticed organically from lived experience | Brainstormed in a "startup idea" session |  
| Target user | Founder IS the user | Founder imagines what "people" might want |  
| Market | Small but passionate initial users | Huge abstract TAM, no specific users |  
| Competition | Exists but nothing is good enough | No competition (possibly no demand) |  
| Feeling | "Why hasn't someone built this?" | "Wouldn't it be cool if..." |  
| Effort | Involves schlep others avoid | Easy/fun/technical-only problem |

\#\#\# QUOTABLE MOMENTS

\- "Live in the future, then build what's missing"  
\- "The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas"  
\- "The best startup ideas are the ones that seem like bad ideas to most people but are actually good ideas"  
\- "It's exceptionally rare for startups to be killed by competitors — so rare that you can almost discount the possibility"  
\- "Turn off your schlep filter. Turn off your unsexy filter"  
\- "If you have a prepared mind, you'll see startup ideas everywhere"

# PAUL GRAHAM — "STARTUP \= GROWTH

\#\# SOURCE 3: PAUL GRAHAM — "STARTUP \= GROWTH"

\#\#\# SOURCE INFO  
\- \*\*Author:\*\* Paul Graham  
\- \*\*Authority Level:\*\* PRIMARY — HIGHEST  
\- \*\*Date:\*\* September 2012  
\- \*\*URL:\*\* paulgraham.com/growth.html

\#\#\# CORE THESIS

\*\*"A startup is a company designed to grow fast. Being newly founded does not in itself make a company a startup. Nor is it necessary for a startup to work on technology, or take venture funding, or have some sort of 'exit.' The only essential thing is growth."\*\*

\#\#\# THE GROWTH RATE BENCHMARKS (Directly from PG)

| Weekly Growth Rate | Assessment |  
|-------------------|------------|  
| 10%+ per week | Exceptionally well — top tier |  
| 5-7% per week | Good — solid YC-caliber growth |  
| 1% per week | Sign you haven't figured out what you're doing |  
| Flat or negative | Major concern — pre-PMF or wrong market |

\#\#\# THE COMPOUND GROWTH MATH

A company making $1,000/month:  
\- At 1% weekly growth → $7,900/month after 4 years  
\- At 5% weekly growth → $25 MILLION/month after 4 years  
\- Small percentage differences compound into completely different outcomes

\#\#\# GROWTH AS DECISION COMPASS

\- Pick a target growth rate and use it for EVERY decision  
\- Should you go to that conference? Only if it helps hit the growth rate  
\- Should you build that feature? Only if it helps hit the growth rate  
\- Should you hire that person? Only if they help hit the growth rate  
\- "Judging yourself by weekly growth doesn't mean you can look no more than a week ahead"

\#\#\# THREE PHASES OF STARTUP GROWTH (S-Curve)

1\. \*\*Phase 1:\*\* Little or no growth (finding PMF)  
2\. \*\*Phase 2:\*\* Rapid growth (PMF achieved, scaling)  
3\. \*\*Phase 3:\*\* Growth levels off (market saturation)

\#\#\# WHY STARTUPS WORK ON TECHNOLOGY

\- Not because technology is inherently valuable  
\- Because technology produces rapid change, and rapid change creates new opportunities  
\- "Ideas for fast growing companies are so rare that the best way to find new ones is to discover those recently made viable by change"

\#\#\# WHAT MAKES SOMETHING VENTURE-SCALE

Two requirements for massive growth:  
\- \*\*(a)\*\* Make something lots of people want  
\- \*\*(b)\*\* Reach and serve ALL those people

A barbershop satisfies (a) but fails (b). Software satisfies both because serving one more user costs essentially nothing.

\#\#\# QUOTABLE MOMENTS

\- "If you want to understand startups, understand growth"  
\- "A good growth rate during YC is 5-7% a week"  
\- "If you can hit 10% a week you're doing exceptionally well"  
\- "If you can only manage 1%, it's a sign you haven't yet figured out what you're doing"  
\- "Growth drives everything in this world"

\#\#\# THE COMPLETE 18 MISTAKES

| \# | Mistake | Why It Kills | Red Flag Signal |  
|---|---------|-------------|-----------------|

| 4 | \*\*Derivative Idea\*\* | "Twitter for pets" — copying a formula without the underlying insight | "We're Uber for X" without understanding why Uber worked |  
| 5 | \*\*Obstinacy\*\* | Refusing to adapt when users are telling you something different | Founder ignores user feedback, doubles down on original vision |  
| 6 | \*\*Hiring Bad Programmers\*\* | Can't tell good from bad, so product quality degrades | Non-technical founders hiring based on credentials, not output |  
| 7 | \*\*Choosing Wrong Platform\*\* | Building on a platform that constrains or dies | Dependent on a single platform/API that could change |  
| 8 | \*\*Slowness in Launching\*\* | Perfecting in isolation, not learning from users | "We'll launch when it's ready" (months/years of building) |  
| 9 | \*\*Launching Too Early\*\* | Rare but possible — alienating users with something truly broken | Product has no core value — not even a "quantum of utility" |  
| 10 | \*\*Having No Specific User in Mind\*\* | Building for "everyone" means building for no one | Can't describe the target user specifically |  
| 11 | \*\*Raising Too Little Money\*\* | Can't reach next milestone, constant fundraising distraction | Raising "just enough" without buffer for mistakes |  
| 12 | \*\*Spending Too Much\*\* | Most common: hiring too fast. Moves to suburbs, has kids | Fancy office, large team, high burn rate pre-PMF |

| 14 | \*\*Poor Investor Management\*\* | Investors add value when things go well, interfere when they don't | Too many investors, losing control of decision-making |  
| 15 | \*\*Sacrificing Users to Profit\*\* | Optimizing revenue before nailing the user experience | Premature monetization that degrades the product |  
| 16 | \*\*Not Wanting to Get Hands Dirty\*\* | Avoiding sales, support, grunt work | Founder won't do unscalable things |  
| 17 | \*\*Fights Between Founders\*\* | \#1 early-stage killer. Destroys execution speed and morale | Unresolved equity splits, unclear roles, recent relationships |

\- Mistakes 6, 7, 8, 9 → \*\*Solution Clarity\*\* deductions

# KEVIN HALE — "HOW TO EVALUATE STARTUP IDEAS"

\#\# SOURCE 5: KEVIN HALE — "HOW TO EVALUATE STARTUP IDEAS" (Deep Extract)

\#\#\# SOURCE INFO  
\- \*\*Author:\*\* Kevin Hale (YC Partner, Wufoo co-founder)  
\- \*\*Authority Level:\*\* PRIMARY  
\- \*\*Context:\*\* Startup School 2019 — this is the DEFINITIVE YC framework for idea evaluation

\#\#\# THE HYPOTHESIS FRAMEWORK

A startup idea \= hypothesis with three components:

\*\*1. PROBLEM (Most important)\*\*  
\- Is it popular? (millions of potential users)  
\- Is it growing? (market expanding)  
\- Is it urgent? (users actively searching for solutions NOW)  
\- Is it expensive to solve today? (willing to pay)  
\- Is it mandatory? (regulation, compliance, necessity)  
\- Is it frequent? (daily/weekly problem \> annual problem)

\*\*2. SOLUTION\*\*  
\- Don't start here\! SISP is the \#1 anti-pattern  
\- Solution should emerge FROM the problem, not the other way around  
\- "Don't start with 'I want to use blockchain.' Start with 'what problem needs solving?'"

\*\*3. INSIGHT (Your unfair advantage)\*\*  
\- Are you 1-in-10 of all people who could solve this? Not "PM at Google" — that's not rare enough. PhD-level domain expertise, specific patent, unique data access  
\- Is the market growing 20%+ annually?  
\- "Why you? Why now? Why this?"

\#\#\# THE TWO STARTUP BELIEFS

Kevin Hale says you need exactly TWO beliefs to invest in a startup:

1\. \*\*"This is a good starting point"\*\* — the initial idea doesn't have to be perfect. It has to be a good STARTING POINT that can evolve  
2\. \*\*"This team can discover the adjacent good idea"\*\* — even if the starting point is wrong, THIS team has the capability to navigate to the right answer

\#\#\# THE YC EXAMPLE (Meta — YC itself as a startup)

Kevin uses YC's own founding as a case study:  
\- \*\*Problem:\*\* Hard for founders to raise money without knowing someone in VC  
\- \*\*Solution:\*\* Invest through an open application — no connections needed  
\- \*\*Unfair advantages:\*\* PG had a large audience of hackers (acquisition), founders were world-class evaluators (expertise), model was genuinely novel (insight)

# MICHAEL SEIBEL — ESSENTIAL STARTUP ADVICE

\#\# SOURCE 6: MICHAEL SEIBEL — ESSENTIAL STARTUP ADVICE (Blog \+ Talks)

\#\#\# SOURCE INFO  
\- \*\*Author:\*\* Michael Seibel (YC CEO/Managing Director, Justin.tv/Twitch co-founder, Socialcam co-founder)  
\- \*\*Authority Level:\*\* PRIMARY — HIGHEST  
\- \*\*Sources:\*\* michaelseibel.com blog posts, Startup Istanbul talk, MIT Q\&A, Startup School

\#\#\# THE 14-SLIDE OPERATING MANUAL (From Startup Istanbul)

Michael compressed everything YC teaches into 14 slides:

1\. \*\*Team \> Idea\*\* — Start with 2-4 committed co-founders who can build  
2\. \*\*Solve daily problems\*\* — The more frequently users face the problem, the faster you learn  
3\. \*\*One hour of market research is enough\*\* — Then build  
4\. \*\*Launch in 8 weeks or less\*\* — Before launch, your growth rate is zero  
5\. \*\*Growth is everything\*\* — Investors care about traction more than pitch decks  
6\. \*\*Talk to users obsessively\*\* — Understand their workflow, not just their stated needs  
7\. \*\*Do things that don't scale\*\* — Manual user acquisition, white-glove onboarding  
8\. \*\*Iterate, don't pivot (usually)\*\* — Same problem, different approach vs. different problem entirely

\#\#\# ON PRODUCT-MARKET FIT (From blog)

\*\*Signs you DON'T have PMF (most common founder self-deception):\*\*  
\- Users sign up but don't come back  
\- Growth requires constant paid acquisition  
\- You're adding features but engagement doesn't improve  
\- Users say "it's nice" but wouldn't care if it disappeared

\*\*Signs you DO have PMF:\*\*  
\- Users pull the product from you — demand exceeds your ability to serve  
\- Organic word-of-mouth growth  
\- Retention cohorts flatten (users stick around)  
\- Users tell other people without being asked

\#\#\# ON EXPLAINING YOUR COMPANY

\- "When you explain your company to someone, never talk about the background of how you got there. Just talk about your product, what it does, and how you plan to grow it"  
\- If you can't explain it in one sentence, you don't understand it well enough  
\- The explanation should teach the listener something — not bore them with buzzwords

\#\#\# THE 90/10 RULE (From Paul Buchheit via Seibel)

\- "How do I get 90% of what I want with only 10% of the work?"  
\- A 90% solution available NOW is much better than a 100% solution that takes months  
\- Most founders resist this because they want perfection  
\- This is the most useful heuristic for deciding what to build next

\#\#\# ON MVP

\- MVP \= the first thing you can give to your first users to see if you deliver ANY value  
\- "Launch something bad, quickly"  
\- Time-box your spec (3 weeks). Cut features to hit the deadline. If nothing seems cuttable, cut important things  
\- "You can talk to your customer all day, but you have no idea whether the thing you want to build can solve their problem. If you put the thing in front of them, you know right away"

\#\#\# QUOTABLE MOMENTS

\- "Launch something bad, quickly"  
\- "Something you know that you think is right and other people think is wrong is normally an idea that works"  
\- "Before launch, your growth rate is zero"  
\- "Great ideas sometimes come up post-YC, post-fundraising. Coinbase today is not who they were when they joined YC"  
\- "Fundamentally you will learn 10x more during building your startup than when you are working towards meeting your resume prerequisites"

# PAUL GRAHAM — "SCHLEP BLINDNESS"

\#\# SOURCE 7: PAUL GRAHAM — "SCHLEP BLINDNESS"

\#\#\# SOURCE INFO  
\- \*\*Author:\*\* Paul Graham  
\- \*\*Authority Level:\*\* PRIMARY  
\- \*\*Date:\*\* January 2012  
\- \*\*URL:\*\* paulgraham.com/schlep.html

\#\#\# CORE THESIS

\*\*There are great startup ideas lying around unexploited right under our noses.\*\* One reason we don't see them is schlep blindness — the unconscious tendency to avoid ideas that involve tedious, unpleasant, or difficult work.

\#\#\# THE KEY INSIGHT

\- "Schlep" \= tedious, unpleasant task (from Yiddish)  
\- Hackers especially dislike schleps — they want to write clever code and watch money roll in  
\- But schleps are what business actually consists of: "A company is defined by the schleps it will undertake"  
\- Your unconscious mind filters OUT ideas that involve painful schleps — you never even consider them  
\- This means the best ideas (the ones nobody is pursuing) are often hiding behind the schleps everyone avoids

\#\#\# THE STRIPE EXAMPLE (Central to the essay)

\- For over a decade, every developer who processed payments online knew how painful it was  
\- Thousands of people knew about this problem  
\- Yet they started recipe sites and local event aggregators instead  
\- Why? Because fixing payments meant dealing with banks, fraud, security, regulations — massive schlep  
\- The Collison brothers (Stripe) took it on anyway → company worth $91.5B+  
\- "Why work on problems few care about and no one will pay for, when you could fix one of the most important components of the world's infrastructure?"

\#\#\# HOW TO OVERCOME SCHLEP BLINDNESS

1\. \*\*Ignorance helps\*\* — "Most successful founders would probably say that if they'd known about the obstacles, they might never have started." This is why young founders often succeed  
2\. \*\*Reframe the question\*\* — Instead of "what problem should I solve?" ask "what problem do I wish someone else would solve for me?"  
3\. \*\*Recognize that scary \= valuable\*\* — "Ambitious ideas are doubly valuable — there's less competition because everyone else has been frightened off"

\#\#\# HOW THIS MAPS TO YOUR VALIDATOR

\*\*Scoring implications:\*\*  
\- If a founder's idea involves significant schlep that others avoid → GREEN FLAG (less competition, real pain point)  
\- If a founder's idea is technically clean but in a crowded consumer space → potential tar pit  
\- "Unsexy but good" ideas should score HIGHER, not lower, on Problem Quality

\#\#\# QUOTABLE MOMENTS

\- "A company is defined by the schleps it will undertake"  
\- "Schleps should be dealt with the same way you'd deal with a cold swimming pool: just jump in"  
\- "Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps. That's schlep blindness"  
\- "Ambitious ideas doubly valuable — less competition because everyone else has been frightened off"

\#\#\# EXAMPLES BEYOND STRIPE

\- \*\*Scale AI (Alexandr Wang):\*\* Data labeling was tedious, unglamorous work nobody wanted → built a $14B company by embracing the schlep  
\- \*\*Uber:\*\* Fighting taxi unions, city regulations, legal battles — massive schlep, massive opportunity  
\- \*\*Shopify:\*\* Making e-commerce easy involved years of boring infrastructure work

# PAUL GRAHAM — "RELENTLESSLY RESOURCEFUL

\#\# SOURCE 8: PAUL GRAHAM — "RELENTLESSLY RESOURCEFUL"

\#\#\# SOURCE INFO  
\- \*\*Author:\*\* Paul Graham  
\- \*\*Authority Level:\*\* PRIMARY  
\- \*\*Date:\*\* March 2009  
\- \*\*URL:\*\* paulgraham.com/relres.html

\#\#\# CORE THESIS

\*\*YC's single-phrase definition of a good founder: "relentlessly resourceful."\*\*

\#\#\# THE DEFINITION

\- Not just "determined" — that implies banging your head against the wall  
\- Not just "smart" — that implies sitting in a chair thinking  
\- \*\*Relentlessly resourceful\*\* \= the combination of determination AND adaptability  
\- "A good metaphor would be running water. You get blocked in one direction, you immediately flow around the obstacle"

\#\#\# WHAT THIS LOOKS LIKE IN PRACTICE

\*\*Relentlessly resourceful founders:\*\*  
\- Don't accept "no" as final — they find another path  
\- Don't wait for permission — they figure it out  
\- Improvise solutions with whatever is available  
\- Move fast, adapt constantly, never freeze  
\- When one approach fails, they try another immediately — no wallowing

\*\*NOT relentlessly resourceful founders:\*\*  
\- Give up when they hit the first wall  
\- Wait for conditions to be perfect before acting  
\- Are "hapless" — passively let things happen to them  
\- Become paralyzed by obstacles  
\- Complain about problems rather than solving them

\#\#\# HOW TO EVALUATE THIS IN YOUR VALIDATOR

\*\*Questions that reveal relentless resourcefulness:\*\*  
\- "How did you get your first users?"  
\- "What's the biggest obstacle you've faced, and how did you solve it?"  
\- "What happened when X went wrong?"  
\- "How did you build this with limited resources?"

\*\*Green flags:\*\*  
\- Creative workarounds to acquire users  
\- Pivoted approach (not idea) when blocked  
\- Built something functional with minimal resources  
\- Found non-obvious solutions to hard problems

\*\*Red flags:\*\*  
\- Waiting for funding to start building  
\- Waiting for the perfect co-founder before beginning  
\- Blaming external factors for lack of progress  
\- Stating obstacles without describing how they addressed them

\#\#\# QUOTABLE MOMENTS

\- "If I could only tell you one thing about a startup founder: relentlessly resourceful"  
\- "Not just determined, not just smart: relentlessly resourceful"  
\- The opposite of relentlessly resourceful is "hapless" — someone to whom things happen, who floats along passively

# PAUL GRAHAM — "BEFORE THE STARTUP"

\#\# SOURCE 9: PAUL GRAHAM — "BEFORE THE STARTUP" (Full depth)

\#\#\# SOURCE INFO

\#\#\# THE COUNTERINTUITIVE TRUTHS

PG's core argument: \*\*Startups are so counterintuitive that following your instincts will lead you astray.\*\* Specifically:

1\. \*\*Trusting instincts about people works; trusting instincts about ideas doesn't\*\* — You can tell if someone is trustworthy/smart. You can't tell if an idea is good by gut feel alone  
2\. \*\*Startup expertise is overrated\*\* — What matters is expertise in your users, not expertise in "how startups work"  
3\. \*\*"Gaming the system" doesn't work\*\* — Trying to hack the funding process or YC application is counterproductive. Just build something people want  
4\. \*\*Starting a startup consumes your life\*\* — It's not entrepreneurship cosplay. It takes over everything — relationships, health, free time. For years  
5\. \*\*Don't try to start a startup\*\* — Instead, get yourself into a position where startup ideas arise organically (i.e., become an expert in a valuable field)

\#\#\# THE "PLAYING STARTUP" WARNING

PG's strongest warning is against founders who are "playing startup":  
\- Going to startup events instead of talking to users  
\- Polishing pitch decks instead of building product  
\- Posting on social media about "the grind" instead of actually grinding  
\- Networking with investors pre-product

\*\*"Playing house is different from having a house."\*\*

\#\#\# ON DOMAIN EXPERTISE

\- "The way to come up with good startup ideas is to take a step back. Instead of trying to think of startup ideas, make yourself into the kind of person who has startup ideas unconsciously"  
\- Become an expert in something valuable → ideas emerge naturally  
\- Best path: work at an existing startup at the frontier of a field  
\- This is why PG advises young people to learn deeply, not to "start a startup"

\#\# SOURCE 10: PAUL GRAHAM — "HOW NOT TO DIE"

\#\#\# SOURCE INFO  
\- \*\*Author:\*\* Paul Graham  
\- \*\*Authority Level:\*\* PRIMARY  
\- \*\*Date:\*\* August 2007  
\- \*\*URL:\*\* paulgraham.com/die.html

\#\#\# CORE THESIS

\*\*The default state of a startup is dead. The main thing you have to do is not die.\*\*

\#\#\# THE "DEFAULT ALIVE" vs "DEFAULT DEAD" FRAMEWORK

This is one of PG's most operationally useful frameworks:

\- \*\*Default alive:\*\* If expenses stay constant and revenue grows at current rate, you become profitable before running out of money  
\- \*\*Default dead:\*\* Under current trajectory, you run out of money before becoming profitable  
\- \*\*Every founder should know which one they are at all times\*\*  
\- If default dead → either cut expenses or increase growth. Don't ignore it

\#\#\# THE THREE MAIN WAYS STARTUPS DIE

1\. \*\*Founders give up\*\* — This is the most common. Not running out of money, but running out of will  
2\. \*\*They build something nobody wants\*\* — The classic. All other deaths funnel through this  
3\. \*\*They run out of money\*\* — Usually because of \#1 or \#2, but sometimes because of overspending

\#\#\# THE RAMEN PROFITABLE MILESTONE

\- Get to "ramen profitable" as fast as possible — enough revenue to cover founders' living expenses  
\- This gives you infinite runway — you can't be forced to die by investors or the market  
\- Changes the power dynamic with investors — you're choosing to raise, not begging to survive  
\- "Once you cross the threshold of profitability, everything changes"

\#\#\# HOW THIS MAPS TO YOUR VALIDATOR

\*\*Traction/Financial Health Assessment:\*\*  
\- Does the founder know their burn rate, runway, and growth rate?  
\- Are they default alive or default dead?  
\- Are they spending on things that don't help build the product?  
\- Is the path to ramen profitability visible?

\#\# SOURCE 11: GUSTAF ALSTRÖMER — PMF & RETENTION BENCHMARKS

\#\#\# SOURCE INFO  
\- \*\*Author:\*\* Gustaf Alströmer (YC Partner, growth expert)  
\- \*\*Authority Level:\*\* PRIMARY  
\- \*\*Context:\*\* Startup School talks, YC office hours content

\#\#\# PRODUCT-MARKET FIT MEASUREMENT

\*\*How YC actually measures PMF:\*\*  
\- PMF is NOT a feeling. It's measurable through retention data  
\- If users keep coming back without being pushed → you have PMF  
\- If users only return because of emails/notifications/discounts → you don't

\#\#\# RETENTION BENCHMARKS (The real numbers)

\*\*Consumer Products:\*\*  
| Metric | Weak | OK | Good | Great |  
|--------|------|-----|------|-------|  
| D1 retention | \<20% | 20-30% | 30-40% | 40%+ |  
| D7 retention | \<5% | 5-10% | 10-20% | 20%+ |  
| D30 retention | \<2% | 2-5% | 5-10% | 10%+ |  
| DAU/MAU | \<10% | 10-20% | 20-30% | 30%+ |

\*\*B2B/SaaS:\*\*  
| Metric | Weak | OK | Good | Great |  
|--------|------|-----|------|-------|  
| Monthly churn | \>10% | 5-10% | 3-5% | \<3% |  
| Net revenue retention | \<80% | 80-100% | 100-120% | 120%+ |  
| Logo retention (annual) | \<70% | 70-85% | 85-95% | 95%+ |

\#\#\# COHORT ANALYSIS — THE TRUTH DETECTOR

\- \*\*Flattening cohort curve\*\* \= PMF exists (users stick around long-term)  
\- \*\*Declining to zero\*\* \= No PMF (all users eventually leave)  
\- \*\*Smiling curve\*\* (users return after dropping off) \= Rare but very strong signal  
\- Don't look at aggregate metrics — they hide the truth. Always look at cohorts

\#\#\# GROWTH CHANNEL ADVICE

1\. Don't try multiple channels at once — pick one and master it  
2\. Most startups that die don't die from lack of growth hacks — they die from not having a product people want  
3\. Fix retention BEFORE you try to grow  
4\. Paid acquisition that doesn't convert to retained users is lighting money on fire

\# CROSS-CUTTING SYNTHESIS: THE COMPLETE SCORING FRAMEWORK

\#\# Dimension 1: PROBLEM QUALITY (25% weight)

\*\*Score 9-10:\*\* Hair-on-fire problem. Users are actively searching for solutions and willing to use broken workarounds. Problem is frequent (daily/weekly), growing, and expensive to solve today. Founder experienced it personally. Market has schlep that others avoid (Source 7).

\*\*Score 7-8:\*\* Clear problem with evidence of demand. Users acknowledge it and would pay. Founder has talked to 10+ potential users. Not a tar pit (Source 1). Not a SISP (Source 5).

\*\*Score 5-6:\*\* Problem exists but is "nice to have." Users would use a solution if easy but aren't actively seeking one. May be a tar pit idea with hidden structural issues.

\*\*Score 3-4:\*\* Problem is vaguely defined. Founder describes it in abstract terms. No evidence users care. May be a solution looking for a problem (SISP).

\*\*Score 1-2:\*\* No real problem. This is a technology demo, a feature, or a derivative idea ("Uber for X"). Founder can't describe specific users who have this problem.

\#\# Dimension 2: FOUNDER FIT (25% weight)

\*\*Score 9-10:\*\* Relentlessly resourceful (Source 8). Deep domain expertise (1-in-10). Has already built and launched something. Co-founders have worked together for years. Can explain idea in one sentence (Source 1).

\*\*Score 7-8:\*\* Strong domain knowledge. Technical co-founder present. Founders committed full-time. Good answer to "why you, why this, why now?" Have talked to many users.

\*\*Score 5-6:\*\* Decent team but gaps. Maybe no technical co-founder, or co-founders just met. Some domain knowledge but not deep. Full-time but haven't launched.

\*\*Score 3-4:\*\* Red flags present. Co-founders met recently. No one can build the product. Playing startup (Source 9). Waiting for funding to begin.

\*\*Score 1-2:\*\* Deal-breaker territory. Solo non-technical founder for technical product. Part-time commitment. Can't explain the idea simply. Blames others for lack of progress.

\#\# Dimension 3: SOLUTION CLARITY (20% weight)

\*\*Score 9-10:\*\* Product exists, users love it. Clear differentiation from competitors. Launched early, iterated fast (Source 6). MVP delivered core value quickly.

\*\*Score 7-8:\*\* Working product with initial users. Clear value proposition. Not over-built — focused on core functionality. Founder understands competitive landscape deeply.

\*\*Score 5-6:\*\* Product in development. Reasonable approach but not yet validated. Some risk of over-building before launching.

\*\*Score 3-4:\*\* No product yet despite months of work (Mistake \#8 from Source 4). Or product is over-engineered for zero users.

\*\*Score 1-2:\*\* SISP — started with technology, looking for a problem. Or product description is incomprehensible. No demo, no prototype, just a deck.

\#\# Dimension 4: MARKET POTENTIAL (15% weight)

\*\*Score 9-10:\*\* Market growing 20%+ annually (Source 5). Clear "why now" — technology or regulation change making this newly possible. Can become venture-scale ($1B+). Small beachhead market the team can dominate first (Source: Thiel, Lecture 5).

\*\*Score 7-8:\*\* Large and growing market. Reasonable "why now." Some competition but nothing dominant. Clear path from niche to broader market.

\*\*Score 5-6:\*\* Market exists but not clearly growing. "Why now" is weak. Competition is established but beatable.

\*\*Score 3-4:\*\* Market is flat or shrinking. No clear "why now." Heavy competition with no differentiator. Or market is too small to be venture-scale.

\*\*Score 1-2:\*\* No identifiable market. Or massive market claim with no specificity ($100B TAM with no path to customers).

\#\# Dimension 5: TRACTION EVIDENCE (15% weight)

\*\*Score 9-10:\*\* Revenue growing 10%+ week-over-week (Source 3). Strong retention — cohorts flatten (Source 11). Organic growth. Default alive (Source 10). Users acquired through unscalable methods that can be scaled later.

\*\*Score 7-8:\*\* 5-7% weekly growth. Some paying users. Decent retention. Clear growth trajectory. Founder knows metrics cold.

\*\*Score 5-6:\*\* Launched with some users but growth is inconsistent. Metrics tracked but not impressive. Pre-revenue but has engagement.

\*\*Score 3-4:\*\* Launched but no real engagement. Or not launched despite months of work. Vanity metrics only (signups, followers, not usage).

\*\*Score 1-2:\*\* No launch, no users, no metrics. Or "traction" consists of friends/family testing the product. Survey responses and hypothetical demand.

\#\# WEEK 1: HOW TO EVALUATE IDEAS — Kevin Hale

\#\#\# SOURCE INFO  
\- \*\*Author:\*\* Kevin Hale (YC Partner, Wufoo co-founder)  
\- \*\*Authority Level:\*\* Primary  
\- \*\*Context:\*\* Startup School 2019

\#\#\# CORE CLAIMS

1\. A startup idea is a \*\*hypothesis\*\* with three parts: Problem, Solution, Insight  
2\. YC's definition of a startup: a company designed to grow very quickly. If not designed for rapid growth, it's a small business (nothing wrong with that)  
3\. There are only two beliefs about startups you need: (a) the idea is a good starting point, and (b) the team is the team that can discover the adjacent good idea  
4\. YC does NOT only fund companies with traction — they fund ideas, even pre-product  
5\. \*\*SISP\*\* (Solution In Search of a Problem) is the most common anti-pattern. Founders fall in love with a technology (blockchain, AI, etc.) and shoehorn a problem into it  
6\. A great idea has an \*\*unfair advantage\*\*: founder expertise (1-in-10 in the world), growing market (20%+ annually), or unique product insight

\#\#\# EVALUATION CRITERIA

\*\*The Hypothesis Framework (Problem → Solution → Insight):\*\*

| Component | Good Signal | Bad Signal |  
|-----------|-------------|------------|  
| \*\*Problem\*\* | Millions of users, growing, urgent, expensive, mandatory, frequent | Niche without growth path, "nice to have", infrequent |  
| \*\*Solution\*\* | Don't start here\! Start with the problem | SISP — built tech then looked for problem |  
| \*\*Insight\*\* | Founder has unique knowledge most people lack | Generic insight anyone could have |

\*\*Unfair Advantages (Kevin Hale's framework):\*\*  
1\. Are you 1-in-10 of all people who could solve this? (PhD-level expertise, not "PM at Google")  
2\. Is the market growing 20%+ per year?  
3\. Do you have a unique product insight from personal experience?  
4\. Have you built a moat? (Network effects, lock-in, technical complexity)  
5\. Can you acquire users cheaply? (Built-in audience, organic channel)

\#\#\# QUOTABLE MOMENTS

\- "SISP: Solution In Search of a Problem. You're an engineer, you're excited about a new technology... and then you go, 'Okay, what kind of problem can I solve now?'" — Kevin Hale  
\- "How can I predict if an investor will like my idea? The answer is really easy. At YC, the definition of a startup is a company that is designed or created to try to grow very quickly" — Kevin Hale  
\- "PG had invested us at just the idea stage, and so I feel fully committed now to always be trying to fund companies who are just at the idea stage" — Kevin Hale

\#\# WEEK 1 (SUPPLEMENT): HOW TO GET AND EVALUATE STARTUP IDEAS — Jared Friedman

\#\#\# SOURCE INFO  
\- \*\*Author:\*\* Jared Friedman (YC Group Partner)  
\- \*\*Authority Level:\*\* Primary  
\- \*\*Context:\*\* Startup School 2022

\#\#\# CORE CLAIMS

1\. \*\*Common Mistake \#1:\*\* Believing you need an amazing idea to get started. Google was the 20th search engine. Facebook was the 20th social network  
2\. \*\*Common Mistake \#2:\*\* Jumping into the first idea without critical evaluation  
3\. \*\*Common Mistake \#3:\*\* Starting with a solution instead of a problem (SISP)  
4\. \*\*Common Mistake \#4:\*\* Believing startup ideas are hard to find. They're easy — real problems are everywhere  
5\. Evaluate ideas on four criteria: \*\*How big? Founder/market fit? How sure are you it's a real problem? Do you have a new insight?\*\*  
6\. There are \*\*bad filters\*\* founders apply that kill good ideas: "seems hard to start", "boring space", "too ambitious", "existing competitors"

\#\#\# IDEA EVALUATION CHECKLIST (From Jared)

\- Are you solving a real problem, or are you a SISP?  
\- Are you in a \*\*tar pit idea\*\*? (Widespread problem, plausible solution, but structural flaw — e.g., "app for making weekend plans")  
\- How acute is the problem? (Brex: credit cards for startups — extremely acute)  
\- Do you have competition? (Dropbox wasn't first, but had a key insight)  
\- Did this recently become possible or necessary? (Checkr: background checks modernized by regulation change)  
\- Are there good proxies? (Rappi: DoorDash model proven in US, applied to Latin America)  
\- Would you work on this for years even if it didn't succeed?  
\- Does this have founder/market fit for YOUR team specifically?

\#\#\# 7 RECIPES FOR GENERATING STARTUP IDEAS

1\. \*\*Best recipe:\*\* Start with what your team is uniquely good at. What unfair advantage do you have?  
2\. What do you wish someone else would build for you?  
3\. What would you be excited to work on for 10 years? (Warning: can lead astray — passion without business viability)  
4\. What has changed in the world recently that makes something newly possible?  
5\. Find successful companies and look for new variants (Warning: often produces SISPs like "Uber for X")  
6\. Crowdsource by asking domain experts for problems they want solved (works best when asking other founders)  
7\. Look for broken industries ripe for disruption (Warning: poor founder/market fit if you don't know the industry)

\#\#\# BAD FILTERS (Reasons founders wrongly reject good ideas)

1\. "Seems hard to get started" → Best ideas often are hard to start (schlep blindness)  
2\. "Boring space" → Unsexy ideas often have less competition and real demand  
3\. "Too ambitious" → Ambition is a feature, not a bug  
4\. "Existing competitors" → Almost every great company had competitors

\#\#\# QUOTABLE MOMENTS

\- "It doesn't matter if something is a good startup idea for someone else if it's not a good idea for YOUR team" — Jared Friedman  
\- "Tar pit ideas: widespread problem, plausible sounding solution, but structural reason why it's much harder than it looks" — Jared Friedman  
\- "The best way to have startup ideas is to notice them organically. There are great startup ideas all around you, and when you have a prepared mind, you see them everywhere" — Jared Friedman  
\- "First, Google the idea to understand how other people have failed before" — Jared Friedman (practical advice on tar pit avoidance)

\#\# WEEK 2: HOW TO TALK TO USERS — Eric Migicovsky

\#\#\# SOURCE INFO  
\- \*\*Author:\*\* Eric Migicovsky (YC Partner, Pebble founder)  
\- \*\*Authority Level:\*\* Primary (YC Partner)  
\- \*\*Context:\*\* Startup School 2019

\#\#\# CORE CLAIMS

1\. The three most common errors when talking to users: (a) talking about your idea instead of their life, (b) asking leading questions, (c) talking to anyone who will listen instead of your target user  
2\. You should be talking to users CONSTANTLY, not just at the beginning  
3\. Listen for what users DO, not what they SAY they would do. Behavior \> stated preference

\#\#\# 5 ESSENTIAL QUESTIONS FOR USER INTERVIEWS

1\. "What is the hardest part about \[doing the thing your product helps with\]?"  
2\. "Tell me about the last time you encountered this problem?"  
3\. "Why was that hard?"  
4\. "What, if anything, have you done to solve this problem?"  
5\. "What don't you love about the solutions you've tried?"

\#\#\# EVALUATION CRITERIA

\*\*Good signals:\*\*  
\- Founder has conducted 10+ user interviews using open-ended questions  
\- Founder can tell specific stories about user pain points (names, situations, details)  
\- Users are spending time or money on bad workarounds — validates the problem exists  
\- Founder can distinguish between "users say they want X" and "users actually do Y"

\*\*Bad signals:\*\*  
\- Founder's user research is a SurveyMonkey link sent to friends  
\- Founder leads with "Would you use an app that...?" (hypothetical, leading)  
\- Founder conflates "people said they'd pay for it" with actual validation  
\- No specifics — just "everyone I talked to thinks it's a great idea"

\#\# WEEK 2 (SUPPLEMENT): HOW TO PLAN AN MVP — Michael Seibel

\#\#\# SOURCE INFO

\- \*\*Authority Level:\*\* Primary (highest)

\#\#\# CORE CLAIMS

2\. \*\*Launch quickly.\*\* This has been YC's core advice for 15+ years. It continues to be great advice

\- Founder launched something within weeks, even if ugly/broken  
\- Founder can articulate what they learned from their MVP  
\- Iterating quickly — weekly or biweekly releases  
\- Founder distinguished between iterating (same problem, different approach) and pivoting (different problem)

\*\*Bad signals:\*\*  
\- Founder has been building for 6+ months without showing anything to users  
\- Founder is "almost ready to launch" and has been saying that for months  
\- Founder added features before validating the core value proposition  
\- Founder built a fully polished product before testing the hypothesis

\#\# WEEK 5: HOW TO SET KPIs — Adora Cheung

\#\#\# SOURCE INFO  
\- \*\*Author:\*\* Adora Cheung (YC Partner)  
\- \*\*Authority Level:\*\* Primary  
\- \*\*Context:\*\* Startup School 2019

\#\#\# CORE CLAIMS

1\. KPI \= Key Performance Indicator. Choose ONE primary metric and 3-5 secondary metrics  
2\. Primary metric should be either \*\*revenue\*\* or \*\*active users\*\* — pick based on your business model  
3\. For most startups: if you charge, primary \= revenue. If free/freemium, primary \= active users  
4\. "Active" must be defined honestly — DAU/WAU/MAU depends on your product's natural usage frequency  
5\. Set weekly goals. Good growth \= 5-7% week-over-week. Great growth \= 10%+ WoW  
6\. Biotech/hardtech: KPIs are different — focus on technical milestones that de-risk the core tech

\#\#\# EVALUATION CRITERIA

\*\*Good signals:\*\*  
\- Founder can instantly tell you their primary metric and current value

\- Founder thinks raising money \= success

\#\# LECTURE 10: CULTURE — Brian Chesky, Alfred Lin

\#\#\# SOURCE INFO  
\- \*\*Authors:\*\* Brian Chesky (Airbnb CEO), Alfred Lin (Sequoia, former Zappos COO)  
\- \*\*Authority Level:\*\* Tertiary  
\- \*\*Context:\*\* Stanford CS183B

\#\#\# CORE CLAIMS

1\. Culture \= the shared beliefs and values that determine how you make decisions when no one is watching  
2\. Your first 10 hires define your culture permanently. Hire missionaries, not mercenaries  
3\. Core values should be lived, not laminated. If a value doesn't influence a firing decision, it's not real  
4\. The founders ARE the culture. Whatever the founders tolerate becomes the norm  
5\. Culture is a competitive advantage — it determines how fast you can move and how well you recruit

\#\#\# EVALUATION CRITERIA

\*\*Good signals (Culture/Team):\*\*  
\- Founders can articulate their values and give specific examples of living them  
\- First hires were made based on values alignment, not just skills  
\- Team has rituals, practices, or habits that reinforce core beliefs  
\- Founders have fired someone who was high-performing but culturally misaligned

\*\*Bad signals (Culture/Team):\*\*  
\- Founders talk about culture generically ("we're like a family")  
\- No clear values beyond "work hard" or "move fast"  
\- Hires are made purely on technical ability with no values screen  
\- Culture is aspirational rather than descriptive of current behavior

\#\#\# QUOTABLE MOMENTS

\- "Don't f\*\*\* up the culture" — Brian Chesky (what Peter Thiel told him)  
\- "Culture is simply a shared way of doing something with passion" — Brian Chesky  
\- "Your culture is who you hire, who you fire, and who you promote" — Alfred Lin (paraphrased)

\#\#\# EXAMPLES GIVEN

\- \*\*Airbnb:\*\* Chesky spent months on the first hire. Asked candidates: "If you had one year left to live, would you still take this job?"  
\- \*\*Zappos:\*\* Built entire company around culture. Would offer new employees $2,000 to quit — if they took it, they weren't a cultural fit  
\- \*\*Apple:\*\* Steve Jobs' obsessive culture of design and simplicity permeated every decision
