# YC Knowledge Base: Idea Generation and Evaluation
*Source: Startup School - "How to Get and Evaluate Startup Ideas" (Jared Friedman)*

## 1. Common Mistakes
- **SISP (Solution in Search of a Problem):** Starting with a technology (like AI) and looking for a problem. Real startups start with a high-quality, specific, and tractable problem.
- **Tar Pit Ideas:** Widespread problems that seem easy to solve but have hidden structural barriers (e.g., social coordination/meet-up apps). 
  - *Test:* Google the idea. If dozens have failed before, find out why.
- **The Waiting Game:** Picking the first idea without thought OR waiting for the "perfect" idea. Aim for a "good starting point" that can morph.

## 2. The 10-Question Evaluation Framework
1. **Founder-Market Fit:** Are you the right team? (e.g., PlanGrid founders in construction).
2. **Market Size:** Billion-dollar current market OR a small, rapidly growing one (e.g., Coinbase in 2012).
3. **Problem Acuteness:** Is the alternative "literally nothing"? (e.g., Brex for startup credit cards).
4. **Competition:** Most good ideas have it. If entrenched, you need a specific new insight.
5. **Personal Want:** Do you or people you know personally want this?
6. **Timing (Why Now?):** Recent technology, regulatory, or world changes (e.g., Checkr and the gig economy).
7. **Proxies:** Large companies doing similar things in different markets/regions (e.g., Rappi as a proxy for DoorDash in LATAM).
8. **Passion:** Can you work on this for 10 years? Boring spaces often grow on founders once successful.
9. **Scalability:** Pure software vs. services/agencies. Avoid high-skill human labor dependencies.
10. **Idea Space:** A category of ideas (e.g., Fintech Infrastructure). Some spaces have historically higher "hit rates" for billion-dollar companies.

## 3. "Good" Bad Ideas
These qualities scare off lazy founders, leaving opportunities on the table:
- **Schlep Blindness:** Ideas that are hard to get started due to "schleps" (banking deals, regulatory hurdles, manual nitty-gritty). Stripe is the classic example.
- **Boring Spaces:** Payroll (Gusto) or Tax software. High hit rates because they are ignored by founders seeking "fun" ideas.
- **Existing Competitors:** Proves demand. Dropbox succeeded as the 20th player because existing UIs sucked and didn't solve the core problem (syncing).

## 4. Generation Recipes
- **Expertise:** Start with what your team is uniquely good at.
- **Unusual Position:** Problems seen through rare life experiences (e.g., VetCove).
- **Recent Change:** Pivoting based on world shifts (e.g., Gather.town and COVID).
- **Industry Disruption:** Looking for big, broken industries.
