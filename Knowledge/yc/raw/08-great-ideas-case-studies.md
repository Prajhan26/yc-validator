# YC Knowledge Base: Great Ideas & Contrarian Thinking
*Source: Michael Seibel & Dalton Caldwell - "Where do great startup ideas come from?" (Airbnb, Coinbase, Stripe Case Studies)*

## 1. Characteristics of Great Startup Ideas
- **10x Better:** They offer a 10x improvement over existing solutions, even if those solutions are dominant.
- **Contrarian:** Most people (especially non-users or "experts") think the ideas are horrible or impossible.
  - *Quote:* "In all three of these cases, these folks had the problem, they had experience, and in hindsight there was an obvious opportunity to make something 10x better. But most people thought they were idiots. And that's probably the overarching theme—they had to be contrarian." — Michael Seibel
- **Underestimated Potential:** The opportunities turn out to be far bigger than the founders initially knew.
- **Timing is Key:** Not necessarily about being the first mover, but about a moment when existing products are so bad that a 10x better solution is feasible.
- **Solving Own Problem:** Founders often build solutions for their own acute, personal problems (e.g., Airbnb founders needing rent money).
- **Execution is Paramount:** Talk is cheap; execution is hard and expensive. These companies succeeded because they *did* the hard work.

## 2. Investor/Expert Skepticism (Red Flags for Founders)
- **Ignore Non-Users:** Don't "give a shit" if experts or investors think your idea is bad, especially if they don't experience the problem or use the existing product.
- **Market Size Underestimation:** Don't be discouraged by initial claims of a "tiny market." Transformational technologies often create unforeseen use cases, expanding the market.
- **Crowded Market Fallacy:** A crowded market can be a good sign, proving demand. The key is a specific insight into what competitors missed or did poorly.

## 3. Case Studies

### Airbnb
- **Problem:** Existing options (VRBO, Couchsurfing) didn't facilitate payments, charged hosts upfront, or were "uncool" to commercialize.
- **10x Insight:** Facilitating payments directly, asset utilization (renting out spare rooms) during the financial crisis.
- **Contrarian Aspect:** Charging for a community-based service was seen as "impure" and unappealing (staying in a stranger's home).
- **Initial Skepticism:** Investors thought it was "dumb," unappealing, and against the "pay it forward" ethos of Couchsurfing.

### Coinbase
- **Problem:** Buying Bitcoin circa 2011-2012 was extremely difficult, risky (money orders, Mt. Gox hacks).
- **10x Insight:** Simple, non-sketchy way to buy Bitcoin with a debit card in the US without getting hacked. Portable, online gold.
- **Contrarian Aspect:** Bitcoin was seen as a tiny market, a bubble, synonymous with fraud. Getting bank deals was "impossible."
- **Initial Skepticism:** Brian himself initially underestimated the core problem in his YC application. Investors rejected it on Demo Day.

### Stripe
- **Problem:** Accepting credit cards online pre-Stripe was "gnarly" (like applying for a mortgage, 30-page contracts, 3 months setup).
- **10x Insight:** Developer-first payments (beautiful docs, API), making a painful process seamless.
- **Contrarian Aspect:** "Two teenagers in banking," highly regulated space, seemingly impossible bank deals, PayPal already existed.
- **Initial Skepticism:** Seen as a "suicide mission." Was the "most expensive product in the market," yet people were desperate for it.

## 4. Key Takeaways for Founders
- Don't "give a shit" if there's an existing product if users hate it.
- Don't "give a shit" if experts (who aren't users) think your idea is bad.
- Be careful about initial market math; markets for transformational tech often grow unexpectedly.
