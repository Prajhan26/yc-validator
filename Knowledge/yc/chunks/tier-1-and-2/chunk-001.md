# DALTON CALDWELL — YC APPLICATION & INTERVIEW

\#\# SOURCE 1: DALTON CALDWELL — YC APPLICATION & INTERVIEW SYSTEM

\#\#\# SOURCE INFO  
\- \*\*Author:\*\* Dalton Caldwell (YC Managing Director, Head of Admissions, 21 batches, 6,500+ office hours, reviewed tens of thousands of applications)  
\- \*\*Authority Level:\*\* PRIMARY — HIGHEST for application evaluation  
\- \*\*Sources:\*\* TechCrunch interview, YC Interview Guide, Lenny's Podcast, Startup School talk

\#\#\# HOW YC ACTUALLY READS APPLICATIONS

\*\*First thing Dalton looks at: THE TEAM\*\*  
\- "The first thing I look at when I read an application is the team. What I'm looking for is technical excellence on the team"  
\- Dalton's \#1 tip: make it clear at least one person can build the product  
\- He reads dozens, sometimes hundreds of applications with the same idea — what differentiates is the TEAM, not the idea

\*\*What makes YC stop reading:\*\*  
\- Lying or misrepresenting (instant kill — "You wouldn't believe the stuff people put in applications")  
\- No one technical on the team  
\- Can't explain what the company does simply  
\- Overly long, novel-length applications (conciseness wins)

\*\*What makes YC keep reading:\*\*  
\- Clear evidence someone can build the product  
\- Progress since applying (launched, got users, grew revenue)  
\- Thoughtful answer to "Why did you pick this idea?"  
\- Hacked a non-computer system (shows resourcefulness — "a strong answer can rescue a weak application")

\*\*Application truths most founders don't know:\*\*  
\- More than half of each batch applied before and got rejected at least once  
\- You don't need warm intros — the system is designed for complete strangers  
\- Don't obsess over the application — "I haven't seen a great correlation between folks that spend tons of time on their application and actually getting in"  
\- Cold emails and decks sent outside the application are archived unread  
\- \~1/3 of each batch applied in a previous batch — showing progress between applications is one of the strongest positive signals

\#\#\# YC INTERVIEW — THE ACTUAL FORMAT

\*\*Structure:\*\*  
\- 10 minutes over Zoom  
\- All founders must be present  
\- 2-3 YC Partners on the call  
\- Partners have already read the application

\*\*The first question (always):\*\* "What is your company working on?"  
\- This is the most basic investor question — and most founders can't answer it clearly  
\- Explain in a few simple, jargon-free sentences  
\- Don't prepare speeches or screencasts — they want a conversation

\*\*What interviewers actually assess:\*\*  
\- Can you explain what you do simply?  
\- Do you know your users and metrics cold?  
\- Are you honest about challenges? (Candid \> glib dismissal of problems)  
\- Can you demo what you've built?  
\- Do all founders participate, or does one person dominate?  
\- Are you earnest and natural, or rehearsed and awkward?

\*\*Questions YC asks if you've launched:\*\*  
\- Where do new users come from?  
\- What is your growth like?  
\- How much are your users using the product, and do they stick around?  
\- What are your unit economics?  
\- What makes new users try you? Why do the reluctant ones hold back?  
\- What are the top things users want?  
\- What has surprised you about user behavior?

\*\*Counter-intuitive interview advice:\*\*  
\- Don't do mock interview prep — it often makes founders worse (over-rehearsed, awkward)  
\- Don't prepare presentations — they want spontaneous conversation  
\- Instead of rehearsing, MAKE PROGRESS — launch, get users, grow between application and interview  
\- "Demonstrating you can move fast and make quick progress is the most surefire way to impress"  
\- It's OK to not know an answer — "A smart person trying sincerely to answer an unexpected question can lead to a great discussion"

\#\#\# DALTON ON FOUNDER TRAITS

\*\*The defining trait of successful founders:\*\*  
\- Not a specific personality type  
\- Profound self-belief — unwavering conviction in their startup's potential  
\- Refuse to accept failure — they persuade others of their inevitable success  
\- "Just don't die" mindset

\*\*On tar pit ideas (Dalton's key concept):\*\*  
\- Ideas that receive initial positive feedback but have hidden structural problems  
\- They SOUND good, people nod along, but there's a reason nobody has succeeded  
\- Examples: social apps for niche demographics, "Uber for X" without network effects  
\- First defense: Google the idea and study why others failed
