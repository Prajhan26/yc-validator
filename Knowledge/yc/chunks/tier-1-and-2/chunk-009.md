\#\#\# THE COUNTERINTUITIVE TRUTHS

PG's core argument: \*\*Startups are so counterintuitive that following your instincts will lead you astray.\*\* Specifically:

1\. \*\*Trusting instincts about people works; trusting instincts about ideas doesn't\*\* — You can tell if someone is trustworthy/smart. You can't tell if an idea is good by gut feel alone  
2\. \*\*Startup expertise is overrated\*\* — What matters is expertise in your users, not expertise in "how startups work"  
3\. \*\*"Gaming the system" doesn't work\*\* — Trying to hack the funding process or YC application is counterproductive. Just build something people want  
4\. \*\*Starting a startup consumes your life\*\* — It's not entrepreneurship cosplay. It takes over everything — relationships, health, free time. For years  
5\. \*\*Don't try to start a startup\*\* — Instead, get yourself into a position where startup ideas arise organically (i.e., become an expert in a valuable field)

\#\#\# THE "PLAYING STARTUP" WARNING

PG's strongest warning is against founders who are "playing startup":  
\- Going to startup events instead of talking to users  
\- Polishing pitch decks instead of building product  
\- Posting on social media about "the grind" instead of actually grinding  
\- Networking with investors pre-product

\*\*"Playing house is different from having a house."\*\*

\#\#\# ON DOMAIN EXPERTISE

\- "The way to come up with good startup ideas is to take a step back. Instead of trying to think of startup ideas, make yourself into the kind of person who has startup ideas unconsciously"  
\- Become an expert in something valuable → ideas emerge naturally  
\- Best path: work at an existing startup at the frontier of a field  
\- This is why PG advises young people to learn deeply, not to "start a startup"

\#\# SOURCE 10: PAUL GRAHAM — "HOW NOT TO DIE"

\#\#\# SOURCE INFO  
\- \*\*Author:\*\* Paul Graham  
\- \*\*Authority Level:\*\* PRIMARY  
\- \*\*Date:\*\* August 2007  
\- \*\*URL:\*\* paulgraham.com/die.html

\#\#\# CORE THESIS

\*\*The default state of a startup is dead. The main thing you have to do is not die.\*\*

\#\#\# THE "DEFAULT ALIVE" vs "DEFAULT DEAD" FRAMEWORK

This is one of PG's most operationally useful frameworks:

\- \*\*Default alive:\*\* If expenses stay constant and revenue grows at current rate, you become profitable before running out of money  
\- \*\*Default dead:\*\* Under current trajectory, you run out of money before becoming profitable  
\- \*\*Every founder should know which one they are at all times\*\*  
\- If default dead → either cut expenses or increase growth. Don't ignore it

\#\#\# THE THREE MAIN WAYS STARTUPS DIE

1\. \*\*Founders give up\*\* — This is the most common. Not running out of money, but running out of will  
2\. \*\*They build something nobody wants\*\* — The classic. All other deaths funnel through this  
3\. \*\*They run out of money\*\* — Usually because of \#1 or \#2, but sometimes because of overspending

\#\#\# THE RAMEN PROFITABLE MILESTONE

\- Get to "ramen profitable" as fast as possible — enough revenue to cover founders' living expenses  
\- This gives you infinite runway — you can't be forced to die by investors or the market  
\- Changes the power dynamic with investors — you're choosing to raise, not begging to survive  
\- "Once you cross the threshold of profitability, everything changes"

\#\#\# HOW THIS MAPS TO YOUR VALIDATOR

\*\*Traction/Financial Health Assessment:\*\*  
\- Does the founder know their burn rate, runway, and growth rate?  
\- Are they default alive or default dead?  
\- Are they spending on things that don't help build the product?  
\- Is the path to ramen profitability visible?

\#\#\# QUOTABLE MOMENTS

\- "The default state of a startup is dead"  
\- "So I'll tell you now: bad shit is coming. It always is in a startup"  
\- "Startups rarely die in mid-keystroke. They die because the founders give up"  
\- "If you can just avoid dying, you get rich"
