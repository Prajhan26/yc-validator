\#\# LECTURE 9: HOW TO RAISE MONEY — Marc Andreessen, Ron Conway

\#\#\# SOURCE INFO  
\- \*\*Authors:\*\* Marc Andreessen (a16z), Ron Conway (SV Angel)  
\- \*\*Authority Level:\*\* Tertiary (top VCs, not YC partners but deeply connected)  
\- \*\*Context:\*\* Stanford CS183B

\#\#\# CORE CLAIMS

1\. Raise money as quickly as possible, then get back to work. Fundraising flattens your growth curve  
2\. Valuation is NOT equal to success or even probability of success  
3\. Investors are pattern-matching machines — they look for founders who remind them of previous winners  
4\. The best signal to investors is organic growth and traction, not a polished pitch deck  
5\. Network effects and "why now" are the two things that get investors most excited  
6\. Most money should stay in the bank. The money you raise is NOT your money — fiduciary duty

\#\#\# EVALUATION CRITERIA

\*\*Good signals (Fundraising Readiness):\*\*  
\- Company has real metrics to show (revenue, growth rate, retention)  
\- Founder can explain the business in one sentence  
\- Clear answer to "why now?" — what changed recently that makes this possible  
\- Founder has conviction and won't pivot based on investor feedback alone

\*\*Bad signals (Fundraising Readiness):\*\*  
\- Founder leads with the fundraise amount instead of the product  
\- No metrics — all projections  
\- Founder changes the pitch based on whatever the last investor said  
\- Founder thinks raising money \= success

\#\#\# QUOTABLE MOMENTS

\- "The best companies raise money very quickly. The second best companies never raise money at all" — paraphrased from PG  
\- "We look at the team, the product, and the market — in that order" — Ron Conway  
\- "Great products sell themselves. If your product is great, investors will find you" — Marc Andreessen (paraphrased)

\#\#\# EXAMPLES GIVEN

\- \*\*Airbnb, Dropbox, Twitch:\*\* All raised on tiny initial valuations. Valuation ≠ success  
\- \*\*Pattern:\*\* The best YC companies often have the easiest fundraises because their traction speaks for itself

\#\# LECTURE 10: CULTURE — Brian Chesky, Alfred Lin

\#\#\# SOURCE INFO  
\- \*\*Authors:\*\* Brian Chesky (Airbnb CEO), Alfred Lin (Sequoia, former Zappos COO)  
\- \*\*Authority Level:\*\* Tertiary  
\- \*\*Context:\*\* Stanford CS183B

\#\#\# CORE CLAIMS

1\. Culture \= the shared beliefs and values that determine how you make decisions when no one is watching  
2\. Your first 10 hires define your culture permanently. Hire missionaries, not mercenaries  
3\. Core values should be lived, not laminated. If a value doesn't influence a firing decision, it's not real  
4\. The founders ARE the culture. Whatever the founders tolerate becomes the norm  
5\. Culture is a competitive advantage — it determines how fast you can move and how well you recruit

\#\#\# EVALUATION CRITERIA

\*\*Good signals (Culture/Team):\*\*  
\- Founders can articulate their values and give specific examples of living them  
\- First hires were made based on values alignment, not just skills  
\- Team has rituals, practices, or habits that reinforce core beliefs  
\- Founders have fired someone who was high-performing but culturally misaligned

\*\*Bad signals (Culture/Team):\*\*  
\- Founders talk about culture generically ("we're like a family")  
\- No clear values beyond "work hard" or "move fast"  
\- Hires are made purely on technical ability with no values screen  
\- Culture is aspirational rather than descriptive of current behavior

\#\#\# QUOTABLE MOMENTS

\- "Don't f\*\*\* up the culture" — Brian Chesky (what Peter Thiel told him)  
\- "Culture is simply a shared way of doing something with passion" — Brian Chesky  
\- "Your culture is who you hire, who you fire, and who you promote" — Alfred Lin (paraphrased)

\#\#\# EXAMPLES GIVEN

\- \*\*Airbnb:\*\* Chesky spent months on the first hire. Asked candidates: "If you had one year left to live, would you still take this job?"  
\- \*\*Zappos:\*\* Built entire company around culture. Would offer new employees $2,000 to quit — if they took it, they weren't a cultural fit  
\- \*\*Apple:\*\* Steve Jobs' obsessive culture of design and simplicity permeated every decision
