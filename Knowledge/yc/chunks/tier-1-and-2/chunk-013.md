# HOW TO START A STARTUP  Sam Altman, Dustin

LECTURE 1: HOW TO START A STARTUP — Sam Altman, Dustin Moskovitz

\#\#\# SOURCE INFO  
\- \*\*Authors:\*\* Sam Altman (YC President), Dustin Moskovitz (Facebook co-founder, Asana CEO)  
\- \*\*Authority Level:\*\* Primary  
\- \*\*Context:\*\* Stanford CS183B opening lecture

\#\#\# CORE CLAIMS

1\. The four critical things that matter in a startup: \*\*Idea, Product, Team, Execution\*\* — in roughly that order for early stage  
2\. It is much more important to build something a small group loves than something a large group merely likes  
3\. Great execution is at least 10x more important and 100x harder than a great idea  
4\. You should almost always start a company because you feel compelled by a particular problem, not because you want to start a company

\#\#\# EVALUATION CRITERIA

\*\*Good signals:\*\*  
\- Founder can explain the idea in one sentence clearly  
\- The idea sounds bad at first but is actually good (contrarian & right)  
\- Product has organic word-of-mouth growth — users voluntarily tell friends  
\- Small set of users who desperately love the product

\*\*Bad signals:\*\*  
\- Idea requires a long explanation to make sense  
\- Idea sounds obviously good to everyone (likely crowded or commoditized)  
\- Growth is purely paid with no organic pull  
\- Users think the product is "fine" but wouldn't care if it disappeared

\#\#\# QUOTABLE MOMENTS

\- "The best companies are almost always mission-driven" — Sam Altman  
\- "You need a market that's going to be big in 10 years" — Sam Altman  
\- "One of the most important tasks for a founder is to make sure the company builds a great product... until you've built a great product almost nothing else matters" — Sam Altman  
\- "The best reason to start a startup is because you can't not do it" — Dustin Moskovitz  
\- "If you join a rocketship, it doesn't matter what seat you get on it" — Sam Altman (on alternative path of joining a great startup)

\#\#\# EXAMPLES GIVEN

\- \*\*Airbnb:\*\* Idea sounded terrible ("strangers sleeping in your house?") — classic "sounds bad, is good"  
\- \*\*Facebook:\*\* Wasn't the first social network; great execution and focus on college students made it work  
\- \*\*Google:\*\* Wasn't the first search engine (roughly 20th); better technology \+ relentless execution
